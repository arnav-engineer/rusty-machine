# IEEE 6-Page Paper Guidelines

## Objective

This document provides a precise, IEEE-aligned structure for writing a 6-page research paper (typical conference format). The focus is on space efficiency, clarity, and technical rigor.

---

## Page Budget (Critical Constraint)

* Total length: **6 pages (strict limit)**
* Two-column IEEE format
* Approximate word capacity: **4500–5500 words including figures/tables**

### Suggested Allocation

* Abstract + Index Terms: ~0.3 page
* Introduction: ~1 page
* Related Work: ~0.5–0.75 page
* Methodology: ~1.5 pages
* Experiments/Results: ~1.5–2 pages
* Discussion: ~0.5 page
* Conclusion: ~0.3 page
* References: ~0.5–1 page

---

## 1. Abstract (150–250 words)

### Requirements

* Single paragraph
* No citations
* No equations

### Content Structure

* Problem statement
* Approach / methodology
* Key quantitative results
* Contribution / impact

### Constraints

* Avoid vague claims
* Avoid narrative tone

---

## 2. Index Terms

* 3–5 keywords
* Use standardized technical terms

Example:

> Machine Learning, Optimization, Neural Networks, Data Augmentation

---

## 3. Introduction (~1 page)

### Purpose

Establish context, define gap, and present contributions.

### Structure

* Context and motivation
* Problem definition
* Limitations of existing approaches
* Gap identification
* Contributions (explicit list)

### Contributions Format

Use a bullet list:

1. Proposed method/model/system
2. Key improvement or novelty
3. Experimental validation or dataset contribution

---

## 4. Related Work (~0.5–0.75 page)

### Objective

Position your work relative to prior research.

### Structure

* Group prior work by approach/category
* Compare methods briefly
* Highlight limitations
* Conclude with positioning of your work

### Constraints

* Keep concise
* Avoid long theoretical exposition

---

## 5. Methodology / Proposed Work (~1.5 pages)

### Content

* System architecture / model design
* Algorithms or workflow
* Mathematical formulation (if applicable)
* Design rationale

### Include

* Diagrams (high value per space)
* Pseudocode (optional)

### Expectations

* Reproducibility
* Technical clarity

---

## 6. Experimental Setup (~0.5 page, can merge with Results)

### Include

* Dataset(s)
* Evaluation metrics
* Baselines
* Implementation details (hardware, libraries)

---

## 7. Results (~1–1.5 pages)

### Content

* Quantitative results (tables/graphs)
* Comparisons with baselines
* Ablation studies (if applicable)

### Rules

* No deep interpretation
* Each figure/table must be referenced in text

---

## 8. Discussion (~0.5 page)

### Content

* Interpretation of results
* Comparison with prior work
* Limitations
* Practical implications

---

## 9. Conclusion and Future Work (~0.3 page)

### Content

* Summary of contributions
* Key findings
* Future research directions

### Constraints

* No new information

---

## 10. Figures and Tables

### Rules

* Figures: caption below
* Tables: title above
* Must be referenced (e.g., Fig. 1)
* Use high information density visuals

---

## 11. References

### Format

* IEEE citation style
* Numbered: [1], [2], ...
* Ordered by appearance

### Quality

* Prefer recent (last 5–7 years)
* Include seminal works if relevant

---

## 12. Writing Constraints

* Use formal, technical language
* Avoid redundancy
* Maximize information density per sentence
* Prefer active voice when clear

---

## 13. Common Failure Points

* Exceeding page limit
* Weak contribution statement
* Overlong literature review
* Poor figure quality
* Missing baseline comparisons
* Lack of quantitative results

---

## Final Checklist
* [ ] Fits within 6 pages
* [ ] Uses IEEE template
* [ ] Includes Index Terms
* [ ] Contributions clearly stated
* [ ] Figures referenced and legible
* [ ] Results are quantitative
* [ ] References properly formatted
